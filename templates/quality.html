Quality: About This Data Set

This is a brief summary about the quality of the data set

Data Source: U.S. Department of Education College Scorecard Data

Documentation: link to data documentation PDF here

The College Scorecard project is designed to increase transparency, putting the power in the hands of students and families to compare how well individual postsecondary institutions are preparing their students to be successful.

This project provides data to help students and families compare college costs and outcomes as they weigh the tradeoffs of different colleges, accounting for their own needs and educational goals.

These data provide insights into the performance of institutions that receive federal financial aid dollars, and the outcomes of the students of those institutions.

(Data Documentation, pp.2)

Judging from the source, the data is accountable.

Is the data complete?

As for completeness, it is necessary to divide the metric into several aspects.

  • Type of information: Yes. According to the documentation, a total of 1837 variables collected in the complete dataset. In this recent cohort dataset, 137 of them were listed, such as basic information about institution (id, location, url, degree type, etc.), admission rate, average SAT scores, student body, family income, finatial aid and so on. These could provide significant help while diciding tradeoffs among schools
  • Missing Data: Many. Only 16% of the schools have SAT, ACT score information. And a large percentage of degrees offered are missing. Also, due to privacy issues, some information regarding finacial situation are not available.

Overall, though the dataset collects many kinds of information, a large part of it is missing values. As a result, it is reasonable to say the dataset has poor completeness.

The reason of the poor completeness, however, is so far unclear. Does most of the colleges not require an SAT or ACT scores? Or they didn't report to the Department of Education? Due to lack of time, it is hard to give a deeper investigation.

Is the data coherent?

Yes. After a simple cleaning of the dataset in Wrangler, which involves eliminating null values in SAT, changing column types, I got reasonable distributions from most of the columns.

Is the data correct?

Judging from the source, and the type of information it tries to collect, I am confident to say that the dataset have high correctness.

Most of the information come from standard tests and finantial data. One would expect SAT and ACT score to have minimum bias. For self-reported finantial information such as family income range, of which the errors could be regard as random, there is also not much room for bias.
